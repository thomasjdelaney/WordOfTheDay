      mid 1600s

      Etymology

      Summary

      A borrowing from Latin.
      Etymon: Latin ēvellere.
      < classical Latin ēvellere to pull or pluck out, to uproot < ē- e- prefix2 + vellere to pluck (see vulsion n.).

      1. 1657–1775
        transitive. To pull or pluck out.
        1. 1657
          Abbas indeed would have the Root thereof evelled [Latin evulsa] on the Forenoon of St. John Baptists day.
          R. Turner, translation of A. Tentzel, 2nd Part Mumial Treat. in translation of Paracelsus, Of Chymical Transmutation 92
        2. 1657
          Neither a great tree can be cut down, nor young shrub evelled, without great danger and peril.
          R. Tomlinson, translation of J. de Renou, Medicinal Materials i, in Medicinal Dispensatory sig. Pp2v
        3. 1657
          The flowers being evelled [Latin euulsis enim floribus], new ones grow not again that year.
          R. Tomlinson, translation of J. de Renou, Pharmaceutrical Shop i, in Medicinal Dispensatory sig. Qqq2v
        4. ?1775
          Nor are the..roots of corruption quite evelled in the renewed heart.
          T. Knight, Balm of Gilead vi. 156
